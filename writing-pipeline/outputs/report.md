# Technical Report: Posterior Inference for a Single Switching Time in Stochastic Dynamics

## 1. Executive Summary

This report studies a single-switch changepoint problem for stochastic trajectories. Each observed sequence is generated by one latent regime before a switch and another latent regime after the switch. The objective is to infer the switching index and to quantify uncertainty over that index, rather than to regress only a single scalar prediction.

The core method is a posterior changepoint model. It encodes the trajectory with an LSTM or Transformer backbone, scores each admissible split by comparing learned summaries of the left and right segments, and normalizes those scores into a discrete posterior over candidate switching times. This structure matters because it separates point accuracy from posterior quality and calibration.

The main conclusions are as follows. On the reference linear benchmark, posterior changepoint modeling is substantially more stable than deterministic regression and segmentation-style neural baselines. Under a mild linear shift involving heavier tails and sparse impulse corruption, the method remains strong. Under stronger linear shifts caused by low separability or irregular observation, performance deteriorates sharply and collapse appears. On the nonlinear Duffing benchmark, the method again performs well in point accuracy and usually remains stable, although one nonlinear stress setting still triggers the collapse diagnostic.

## 2. Problem Statement and Notation

Let the observed trajectory be

```math
x_{1:L} = (x_1, \ldots, x_L),
```

where `L` is the valid trajectory length and `T_h` is the physical observation horizon. There is exactly one latent changepoint `\tau`, and the latent state sequence satisfies `s_t = 0` before the switch and `s_t = 1` after the switch. The regime-occupancy ratio is

```math
\rho = (\rho_0, \rho_1), \qquad \rho_0 = \tau/(L-1), \quad \rho_1 = 1 - \rho_0.
```

The switch is restricted to an interior candidate set. With minimum segment length `m = 16`, the admissible set is

```math
\mathcal{T}(L) = \{\tau : \lfloor 0.2(L-1) \rfloor \le \tau \le \lceil 0.8(L-1) \rceil,\; \tau \ge m,\; (L-1)-\tau \ge m\}.
```

The estimator returns a posterior

```math
q_\theta(\tau \mid x_{1:L}), \qquad \tau \in \mathcal{T}(L),
```

from which the report derives a maximum-a-posteriori estimate, top-three candidate indices, and a 90% highest-posterior-density interval.

Two classes of switching stochastic differential equations are considered. In the linear family,

```math
dX_t = -\eta_j (X_t - \mu)\,dt + \varepsilon_j\, dL_t^{(\alpha_j)}, \qquad j \in \{0,1\},
```

which is a switching Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process. In the nonlinear family,

```math
dX_t = (a_j X_t - b X_t^3 + c_j)\,dt + \varepsilon_j\, dL_t^{(\alpha_j)}, \qquad j \in \{0,1\},
```

which is a switching Duffing process.

## 4. Method

### 4.1 Architecture

The model first maps the input trajectory to latent sequence features

```math
h_{1:L} = f_\theta(x_{1:L}), \qquad h_t \in \mathbb{R}^{64},
```

using either a three-layer LSTM or a three-layer Transformer with dropout 0.1.

For each candidate `\tau \in \mathcal{T}(L)`, the posterior head forms left and right segment summaries,

```math
m_L(\tau) = \frac{1}{\tau}\sum_{t<\tau} h_t,
\qquad
m_R(\tau) = \frac{1}{L-\tau}\sum_{t\ge \tau} h_t,
```

and combines them into the split-specific feature vector

```math
\phi(\tau) = [m_L(\tau),\; m_R(\tau),\; m_L(\tau)-m_R(\tau),\; m_L(\tau) \odot m_R(\tau),\; \tau/(L-1)].
```

A multilayer perceptron produces a candidate score `s_\theta(\tau) = g_\theta(\phi(\tau))`, and the posterior is the masked softmax over admissible candidates,

```math
q_\theta(\tau \mid x_{1:L}) = \frac{\exp(s_\theta(\tau))\,\mathbf{1}[\tau \in \mathcal{T}(L)]}{\sum_{u \in \mathcal{T}(L)} \exp(s_\theta(u))}.
```

This candidate-constrained construction is the main modeling contribution. The model evaluates whether a specific split yields two segments whose learned representations are jointly plausible as pre-switch and post-switch regimes.

### 4.2 Auxiliary heads and training variants

A separate head predicts the occupancy ratio `\rho`, which supplies an auxiliary signal tied to switch location. The strongest baseline configuration uses hard supervision on the true changepoint together with a small auxiliary loss on `\rho`.

The benchmark suite also evaluates three extensions: teacher-guided rank distillation using a plug-in posterior built from simple Ornstein-Uhlenbeck proxies, confidence-gated distillation, and handcrafted segment-statistics augmentation. These variants are useful for diagnosis, but they are not the best-performing final configuration.

## 5. Experimental Protocol

Each split contains 20,000 training trajectories, 2,000 validation trajectories, and 2,000 test trajectories. Neural models are trained for 20 epochs with AdamW, learning rate `10^{-3}`, weight decay `10^{-4}`, batch size 128, and gradient clipping 1.0. Temperature scaling is fitted on the validation set before reporting calibrated posterior metrics. Every neural result is averaged over three random seeds.

The baseline set includes a CUSUM likelihood-ratio detector, an offline Bayesian changepoint detector, a switching hidden Markov model, a plug-in split posterior baseline, deterministic neural regression, and a segmentation-style neural posterior. This breadth is necessary because it distinguishes the value of posterior changepoint modeling from the value of sequence encoding alone.

The primary metrics are mean absolute switching-time error `\mathrm{MAE}_\tau`, tolerant-window detection score `\mathrm{F1}_{0.02}`, calibrated negative log-likelihood, calibrated expected calibration error, top-three hit rate `H_3`, and 90% interval coverage. The report also tracks a collapse indicator,

```math
c = \mathbf{1}\{\mathrm{NLL}_{\mathrm{cal}} > 5.30 \;\text{or}\; r_{\mathrm{teach}} < 0.15 \;\text{or}\; H_3 < 0.025\},
```

where `r_{\mathrm{teach}}` is the rank correlation between the model posterior and the auxiliary teacher posterior. This diagnostic is intentionally strict: a model can have acceptable mean point error and still be judged unreliable if the posterior itself degrades severely.

These threshold values are inherited from the released benchmark scripts, so the report treats them as repository-defined diagnostic constants rather than as universal statistical cutoffs. Because the released tables report seed averages without confidence intervals or error bars, the robustness comparisons should also be read as directional evidence rather than as interval-estimated differences.

## 7. Linear Stress Results

The linear stress benchmark reveals a sharply nonuniform robustness profile. Under Linear Scenario 1, the LSTM posterior model remains strong with `\mathrm{MAE}_\tau = 0.8854`, `\mathrm{F1}_{0.02} = 0.5395`, and zero collapse, substantially outperforming the strongest simple baseline. Under Linear Scenarios 2 and 3, the best posterior rows deteriorate to 3.1398 and 2.7924 MAE, respectively, and collapse appears. In those settings, the CUSUM detector attains lower MAE than the posterior model.

## 9. Ablation and Interpretation

The ablation results show that the strongest transformer-based variant is the simplest one: hard posterior supervision without the more elaborate teacher-guided additions. Its reference-linear metrics are 1.6383 MAE and 0.3015 `\mathrm{F1}_{0.02}`. Teacher-guided rank distillation weakens those numbers slightly to 1.6991 and 0.2895. Confidence-gated distillation weakens them further to 1.7269 and 0.2872. Segment-statistics augmentation is unstable, raising the error to 2.6786 and collapsing in two of three runs.

The primary contribution is therefore the candidate-constrained posterior formulation itself. Teacher-guided distillation and handcrafted segment statistics are better interpreted as exploratory extensions than as the validated core method.

## 10. Overall Conclusion

The full empirical picture is internally consistent once the task is written as posterior changepoint inference. The method is clearly stronger than simpler neural baselines on the reference linear benchmark, genuinely robust to a mild linear stress, clearly vulnerable to stronger linear shifts, and again compelling on nonlinear switching dynamics. The strict collapse diagnostic prevents the report from overstating the strongest nonlinear results.

A self-contained summary of the work should therefore emphasize two points. The first is the modeling idea: compare candidate left and right segments and build a posterior over admissible switch times. The second is the limitation: robustness depends strongly on the mechanism of shift, especially on separability and observation regularity. Those are the most meaningful targets for further improvement.
