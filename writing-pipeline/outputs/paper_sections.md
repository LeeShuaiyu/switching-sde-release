# Switching-Time Posterior Inference for Regime-Switching Stochastic Dynamics

## Abstract

We consider the problem of localizing a single latent regime switch from a short stochastic trajectory while also quantifying uncertainty in the switch location. Instead of predicting only a scalar changepoint estimate, the method constructs a discrete posterior over an admissible interior set of switching indices. The model combines a sequence encoder with a candidate-constrained posterior head that compares learned summaries of the left and right trajectory segments for each feasible split. On the reference linear benchmark, the transformer posterior model reduces switching-time error from 2.1270 to 1.6383 and improves `\mathrm{F1}_{0.02}` from 0.0815 to 0.3015 relative to deterministic neural regression while eliminating collapse; when the backbone comparison is expanded, the best LSTM posterior row reaches 0.6227 MAE and 0.6943 `\mathrm{F1}_{0.02}`. Under mild linear shift, the posterior model remains strong, but under stronger linear shifts it degrades sharply and often collapses. On the nonlinear Duffing benchmark, the LSTM posterior model attains 0.3484 MAE and 0.8367 `\mathrm{F1}_{0.02}` with zero collapse, although one nonlinear stress setting still exhibits a collapse warning despite strong point metrics.

## 1. Introduction

Single-switch localization in stochastic dynamics is not well described as ordinary sequence regression. The object of interest is a latent transition time, and several nearby split locations can remain plausible even after the entire trajectory has been observed. The output should therefore include both a point estimate and an uncertainty distribution over admissible switching times.

We study this problem for scalar trajectories generated by two-state switching stochastic systems. Each sample evolves under one regime before the switch and another regime after the switch. The central question is whether a learned posterior model can localize the switch more accurately and more stably than classical changepoint detectors and simpler neural baselines, especially under controlled distribution shifts.

## 2. Problem Formulation and Notation

Let the observed trajectory be

```math
x_{1:L} = (x_1, \ldots, x_L),
```

where `L` is the number of valid time steps and `T_h` is the physical observation horizon. There is exactly one latent changepoint `\tau`, and the latent state sequence satisfies `s_t = 0` before the switch and `s_t = 1` after the switch. The associated regime-occupancy ratio is

```math
\rho = (\rho_0, \rho_1), \qquad \rho_0 = \tau/(L-1), \quad \rho_1 = 1-\rho_0.
```

The switch is restricted to an interior candidate set rather than the full sequence range. With minimum segment length `m = 16`, the admissible set is

```math
\mathcal{T}(L) = \{\tau : \lfloor 0.2(L-1) \rfloor \le \tau \le \lceil 0.8(L-1) \rceil,\; \tau \ge m,\; (L-1)-\tau \ge m\}.
```

The model predicts a discrete posterior

```math
q_\theta(\tau \mid x_{1:L}), \qquad \tau \in \mathcal{T}(L),
```

from which we derive a maximum-a-posteriori estimate, top-three candidate indices, and a 90% highest-posterior-density interval.

The benchmark family contains two classes of switching stochastic differential equations. In the linear case,

```math
dX_t = -\eta_j (X_t - \mu)\,dt + \varepsilon_j\, dL_t^{(\alpha_j)}, \qquad j \in \{0,1\},
```

which is a switching Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process with state-dependent drift rate `\eta_j`, noise scale `\varepsilon_j`, and stability index `\alpha_j`. In the nonlinear case,

```math
dX_t = (a_j X_t - b X_t^3 + c_j)\,dt + \varepsilon_j\, dL_t^{(\alpha_j)}, \qquad j \in \{0,1\},
```

which is a switching Duffing process.

## 3. Method

### 3.1 Sequence encoder

The input trajectory is first mapped to latent sequence features

```math
h_{1:L} = f_\theta(x_{1:L}), \qquad h_t \in \mathbb{R}^{64},
```

using either a three-layer LSTM encoder or a three-layer Transformer encoder with dropout 0.1. Both backbones are retained because the strongest backbone depends on the benchmark.

### 3.2 Candidate-constrained posterior head

For each admissible candidate split `\tau \in \mathcal{T}(L)`, the model forms left and right segment summaries,

```math
m_L(\tau) = \frac{1}{\tau} \sum_{t<\tau} h_t,
\qquad
m_R(\tau) = \frac{1}{L-\tau} \sum_{t\ge \tau} h_t.
```

These summaries are combined into a split-specific feature vector

```math
\phi(\tau) = [m_L(\tau),\; m_R(\tau),\; m_L(\tau)-m_R(\tau),\; m_L(\tau) \odot m_R(\tau),\; \tau/(L-1)],
```

and a multilayer perceptron produces a candidate score `s_\theta(\tau) = g_\theta(\phi(\tau))`. The posterior is the masked softmax over admissible candidates,

```math
q_\theta(\tau \mid x_{1:L}) = \frac{\exp(s_\theta(\tau))\,\mathbf{1}[\tau \in \mathcal{T}(L)]}{\sum_{u \in \mathcal{T}(L)} \exp(s_\theta(u))}.
```

This structure is central to the method. The model does not classify each time step independently. Instead, it evaluates whether a specific split yields two segments whose learned representations are jointly plausible as pre-switch and post-switch regimes.

### 3.3 Auxiliary quantities and training variants

A separate head predicts the occupancy ratio `\rho`, which supplies an auxiliary signal tied to switch location. The strongest baseline configuration uses hard supervision on the true changepoint together with a small auxiliary loss on `\rho`.

Three extensions are also evaluated. The first performs teacher-guided rank distillation using a plug-in posterior obtained by fitting simple Ornstein-Uhlenbeck proxies to the two sides of each candidate split. The second adds confidence gating so that uncertain teacher targets receive less weight. The third injects handcrafted segment statistics, such as segment means and standard deviations, into the split scorer. These extensions are analytically informative, but they are not the strongest final configuration.

## 4. Experimental Protocol

Each split contains 20,000 training trajectories, 2,000 validation trajectories, and 2,000 test trajectories. Neural models are trained for 20 epochs with AdamW, learning rate `10^{-3}`, weight decay `10^{-4}`, batch size 128, and gradient clipping at 1.0. Temperature scaling is fitted on the validation set before reporting calibrated posterior metrics. Every neural result is averaged over three random seeds.

The baseline set includes a CUSUM likelihood-ratio detector, an offline Bayesian changepoint detector, a switching hidden Markov model, a plug-in split posterior baseline, deterministic neural regression, and a segmentation-style neural posterior baseline. This range of baselines is important because it distinguishes the value of posterior changepoint modeling from the value of sequence encoding alone.

The reported metrics are mean absolute switching-time error `\mathrm{MAE}_\tau`, tolerant-window detection score `\mathrm{F1}_{0.02}` with tolerance equal to 2% of the trajectory length, calibrated negative log-likelihood, calibrated expected calibration error, top-three hit rate `H_3`, and 90% interval coverage. We also track a collapse indicator,

```math
c = \mathbf{1}\{\mathrm{NLL}_{\mathrm{cal}} > 5.30 \;\text{or}\; r_{\mathrm{teach}} < 0.15 \;\text{or}\; H_3 < 0.025\},
```

where `r_{\mathrm{teach}}` is the rank correlation between the model posterior and the auxiliary teacher posterior. This diagnostic is deliberately strict: a model can achieve good mean point accuracy and still be considered unreliable if its posterior quality degrades severely.

The threshold values in this indicator are inherited from the released evaluation scripts, so we interpret them as repository-defined diagnostic constants rather than as universal statistical thresholds. Because the released benchmark tables report seed averages without confidence intervals, the robustness comparisons should also be read as directional rather than as interval-estimated differences.

## 5. Results

### 5.1 Reference linear benchmark

Table 2 reports the reference linear benchmark. The clean controlled comparison is the transformer block: the posterior changepoint model lowers MAE from 2.1270 to 1.6383 and raises `\mathrm{F1}_{0.02}` from 0.0815 to 0.3015 relative to deterministic neural regression, while the collapse rate drops from 1 to 0. The segmentation-style neural posterior also collapses. When the backbone comparison is expanded, the strongest overall neural row is the LSTM posterior model, which reaches 0.6227 MAE and 0.6943 `\mathrm{F1}_{0.02}` with zero collapse, but that broader comparison changes both backbone and head and is therefore evidence about family-level headroom rather than a head-only gain.

### 5.2 Linear stress robustness

The linear stress benchmark exposes a sharply nonuniform robustness profile. Under Linear Scenario 1, the LSTM posterior model remains strong with `\mathrm{MAE}_\tau = 0.8854`, `\mathrm{F1}_{0.02} = 0.5395`, and zero collapse, substantially outperforming the strongest simple baseline. Under Linear Scenarios 2 and 3, the best posterior rows deteriorate to 3.1398 and 2.7924 MAE, respectively, and collapse appears. In those settings, the CUSUM detector attains lower MAE than the posterior model.

### 5.3 Nonlinear robustness

The nonlinear Duffing benchmark is more favorable. Figure 4 already shows that the nonlinear error trend is flatter than in the linear stress suite. On the reference nonlinear benchmark, the LSTM posterior model attains 0.3484 MAE and 0.8367 `\mathrm{F1}_{0.02}` with zero collapse, while the transformer posterior model also remains strong at 0.4991 MAE and 0.7573 `\mathrm{F1}_{0.02}`. On Nonlinear Scenarios 2 and 3, the posterior family keeps zero collapse and remains clearly ahead of the classical baselines in point accuracy.

### 5.4 Ablation study

The ablation study supports a restrained interpretation of the method. Plain hard posterior supervision is the strongest transformer-based configuration with 1.6383 MAE and 0.3015 `\mathrm{F1}_{0.02}`. Teacher-guided rank distillation is slightly weaker at 1.6991 and 0.2895. Confidence-gated distillation drops further to 1.7269 and 0.2872. Injecting handcrafted segment statistics is unstable: the error rises to 2.6786 and the model collapses in two of three runs. The primary empirical contribution is therefore the candidate-constrained posterior formulation itself, not the more elaborate auxiliary variants.

## 6. Discussion

The evidence supports three claims. First, the task is better treated as posterior inference over changepoints than as plain regression. Second, candidate-constrained posterior modeling materially improves nominal linear performance and neural stability. Third, the same formulation remains effective on nonlinear switching dynamics.

The evidence also draws a clear boundary around those claims. The method does not solve severe linear shifts, especially when regime separability deteriorates or the observation process becomes irregular. In addition, strong point accuracy does not guarantee healthy posterior behavior, as shown by Nonlinear Scenario 1. These limitations are not peripheral; they identify the most meaningful targets for future improvement.
